# Reflection
## Critical Analysis of Evaluation Design Challenges
**Author:** Lucas Barrios | **Date:** May 2026

---

## Question 1: What would change in your evaluation design if the data was in Spanish or German?

The benchmark problem gets significantly worse. All three benchmarks identified as methodologically relevant — BFCL, SWE-bench, and GAIA — are English-first by design. BFCL's function documentation and queries were contributed predominantly by English-speaking developers; SWE-bench is built entirely from English GitHub repositories; GAIA's real-world questions assume English-language information retrieval. Adapting them would require either finding multilingual variants (Global-MMLU exists for knowledge tasks, but nothing comparable exists for tool-calling in German or Spanish) or rebuilding the custom prompt set in the target language from scratch. The latter is the correct approach for Kairos specifically — and it's also significantly more expensive, because you can't use synthetic generation shortcuts. The lesson is explicit: you have to read the dataset yourself. For German or Spanish prompts, that means you need a native-fluent reviewer, not just a translation pass.

The new challenges compound quickly. The LLM-as-judge breaks down in non-English contexts in two compounding ways: first, models are trained on far less German and Spanish data than English, so their calibration on quality signals like "professional tone" or "consulting-appropriate register" is weaker and more variable. Second, models favor English responses due to training data imbalance — meaning a judge evaluating a German response may unconsciously penalize it for not reading like an English response. For Kairos specifically, German adds a third layer: formal/informal register (Sie vs. du) matters enormously in a B2B consulting context, and an LLM judge has no reliable way to know which register a given client relationship calls for. Spanish adds regional variation — a response appropriate for a Berlin-based Latin American client may read differently to a Madrid-based one. Verifying quality in these contexts requires human annotators who are not just fluent but domain-literate in consulting German or business Spanish, with explicit annotation guidelines on register and formality.

## Question 2: If asked "is this model AGI-level?" — how would I respond?

The honest answer is: the question isn't well-formed enough to answer. We don't have a stable definition of intelligence for humans, let alone for machines. "AGI-level" as commonly used means something like "performs at or above human level across a broad range of cognitively demanding tasks" — but that definition collapses under scrutiny the moment you specify which tasks, which humans, and which conditions. The models evaluated in this memo score 4.1/5 on five prompts targeting a specific pipeline's failure modes. That tells you something meaningful and narrow. It tells you nothing about whether the model could diagnose a novel architectural failure it hasn't seen, handle a multi-day autonomous research project without human checkpointing, or exercise the kind of judgment a senior consultant would bring to an ambiguous client situation.

The evaluations you'd actually need to approach an AGI claim are not currently achievable in practice. You'd need benchmarks that test genuine transfer — performance on tasks structurally unlike anything in the training distribution. You'd need evaluation of novel problem-solving, not pattern-matching to seen formats. You'd need longitudinal testing across changing contexts, not static snapshots. ARC-AGI was designed with this spirit — tasks that require genuine reasoning rather than memorization — and even frontier models only recently crossed the human baseline there, which immediately prompted a harder version. That cycle repeating is itself the answer: every time a model reaches "human level" on a defined task, the definition shifts. For Kairos's purposes, the right reframe is: "Is this model capable enough for this specific use case, with acceptable failure rates?" That's a question this evaluation can actually answer.

## Question 3: What is the one thing you could not evaluate without a human?

Whether the output would damage a client relationship. Every other criterion in the evaluation framework — factual accuracy, failure transparency, actionability, even tone in the abstract — can be approximated by rules or judge models. But the question of whether a specific response, delivered to a specific client in a specific context, would erode trust is irreducibly human. An LLM judge evaluating "professional tone" is pattern-matching against its training data's notion of professionalism. It cannot know that a particular client has already had a bad experience with a previous vendor who gave vague explanations, making Prompt #3's URL expiry response land as another excuse rather than a diagnosis. It cannot weigh the difference between a response that is technically correct and one that feels correct to someone who just paid for a consulting engagement and got a null result.

Rule-based methods fail here for an obvious reason — there is no regex for trust erosion. LLM-as-judge fails for a subtler reason: it optimizes for what looks good in the abstract, not what lands well in a specific relationship context. The lesson frames this as the core limitation of LLM judges — they are not objective, they have hidden biases, and they cannot replicate the situated judgment a human brings. In practice, the way to incorporate human evaluation for Kairos is a small but structured client feedback loop: after the first 10–15 real research outputs are delivered, a brief structured survey (3 questions, 2 minutes) captures whether the client felt the system communicated clearly when something went wrong. That signal, aggregated over time, is the one ground truth an LLM judge cannot synthesize — and it's also the most important metric for a boutique consulting firm where reputation is the product.
